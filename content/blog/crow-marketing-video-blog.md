That was Project CROW. Cognitive Reasoning Observation Watcher. A unified customer interaction intelligence platform. Six of us, second year uni, eight months of building something that was, genuinely, way more advanced than it had any right to be for a coursework project.

I was handling the design side of things, so I was sitting right in the middle of all of it, the technical decisions on one side, and the question of how an actual human being is supposed to understand any of this on the other. The product was genuinely good. But the second you tried to explain what it *did* to someone who wasn't already neck-deep in enterprise software, you could watch their eyes go somewhere else. Somewhere nicer. Somewhere with less jargon.

That gap between what we built and what people could actually *feel* about it was the thing I couldn't stop thinking about. And with 200+ teams competing and one shot at a showcase, it felt like a problem someone had to actually sit down and solve. That someone ended up being me, not because I was assigned it, just because I couldn't let it go.

---

## The Research Phase Nobody Talks About

First thing I did was spend a day or two just researching how companies actually market technical products like this.

What I found was mostly... nothing useful. Generic product reveal videos. Feature walkthroughs with corporate music. Explainer animations with those little flat-design people who don't look like real humans. The kind of content that's technically informative and emotionally dead.

And the thing is, if we went that route, we'd be exactly like every other team. In a field of 200+, "not terrible" isn't a strategy. Something had to actually make people *feel* something about a product that, on paper, sounds like something your CTO uses to justify a budget line item.

So I started thinking differently about the whole thing.

---

## The Three-Video Plan That Became Something Else

My first actual idea was a 2-week marketing campaign. Three short videos with posts in between. A proper content series. I even wrote the script, it followed a character called Samantha, a product designer who just joined a new company. The idea was to tell a story across three separate videos that slowly introduced CROW in a way that felt human.

I liked it. The team liked it. And then we sat with it for a minute and realized the obvious problem: if even one person misses one video, the whole story falls apart. Three separate pieces means three separate chances for the narrative to break. The chaos of missing context in a story like this isn't dramatic, it's just confusing.

So we scrapped the series format. We talked it through as a team and landed on the idea of combining everything into one short film, and once it was on the table, it was hard to argue against it.

I'll be honest, that felt like an even bigger threat than the original problem. I'd never seen a short film made for a B2B AI platform. Not a real one. Not one that was supposed to be *good*. The few times companies try to do cinematic marketing for technical products it usually ends up feeling like a parody of itself, too polished, too corporate, too much stock footage of people shaking hands.

But the more I sat with it, the more it made sense. One story. One arc. No fragmentation. If we could pull it off, nothing else in that showcase would look remotely like it.

---

## Building the Story

Here's where my UX background actually kicked in in a way I didn't expect.

I'd never written a professional film script before. But I knew how to do creative research. I knew how to build personas. I knew how to sketch out flows and map out user journeys. So I just... applied that to the characters.

I sat with my notebook and sketched out every single scene. Every character. Every emotional beat. The whole story drawn out before a single word of script was written properly. The UX process of "understand the user before you design for them" translated directly into "understand your characters before you write for them."

The story ended up being built around two main characters, Samantha and an Intern. Samantha is a product designer who just got a new product approved, confident bordering on arrogant, expecting a promotion. The Intern is the company's unsung tech genius, three years in the same intern role, does half the company's actual work, and nobody even knows his name.

I made two other characters intentionally undiscovered, the Boss and the Assistant, designed so the audience never pulls focus away from Samantha and the Intern. The Boss specifically I referenced from Tom and Jerry, you know how the woman who owns the house is only ever shown from the waist down? That feeling of authority without a face. I just... applied that. It worked exactly the way I hoped.

The core of the story: Samantha's product fails spectacularly. He's about to get fired. In desperation he finally listens to the Intern, the guy he'd been ignoring for weeks, who pitches him CROW. CROW saves the product. Samantha gets promoted. The Intern is still an intern.

It sounds simple. That's the point. The product needed a human story around it, not a feature list.

---

## The Narrator Problem

Here's where things got complicated in a way I had to think around.

Our team are engineers. Brilliant ones. Not actors. And no matter how good a script is, if the delivery is off, the whole thing reads as awkward student film. Which, fine, technically it is a student film, but that's not how I wanted it to land.

I thought about it for a while and landed on adding a narrator.

Not a traditional narrator. This one isn't named. Isn't explained. Just a voice that knows what's happening and is slightly more entertained by it than they should be. Adding the narrator did two things: it took pressure off the acting for the emotional heavy-lifting, and it gave us a way to highlight exactly what we needed the audience to catch without relying on actors to land the subtext.

That was the third iteration of the script. And that's the one that actually felt right.

## The Planning Phase (AKA Two Days Camped in O2 Cafe)

With the script locked, me and Methika, a teammate who volunteered for the videography side, sat down and started planning shots.

We went through every scene and mapped out every angle. Every camera position. Every transition. What I had in my head needed to be translated into something an actual crew could execute on a single shooting day. We ran test runs to see how specific shots would actually look in frame.

## The Props Problem (Or: Why We Made a Technical Blueprint for a Teddy Bear)

This is where things got genuinely unhinged in the best possible way.

In the film, Samantha's big product is a teddy bear. That's the thing he's proud of, the thing he's betting his promotion on, the thing that fails spectacularly. So when it came to the actual prop, I couldn't just grab any bear off a shelf. It needed to feel like a real product, something Samantha would genuinely care about. So we designed a full technical blueprint for it. Dimensions, materials, construction notes, the whole thing.

## The Shoot Day

11 hours.

That's how long it took to shoot a 5 minute film.

We were moving cameras between three separate floors. Switching costumes between scenes. Setting up lighting on a timeline that was more optimistic than realistic. My job in all of this was to get specific emotions out of people who had never acted before, to match the exact version of the story I had in my head. The team handled everything around it. I just had to worry about that.

Every shot was taken until I was satisfied. I'll be honest, my team had to deal with that. It is what it is. If you're going to spend 8 months building something, the two minutes someone spends watching the thing that introduces it should feel like they were worth it.

But somewhere in the middle of that 11-hour day, in between telling someone to redo a take for the fourth time and figuring out how to get the lighting to look right on a third-floor landing, I felt like a director. An actual one. Not in a pretentious way. Just in the way where you realize you have a clear picture of what you want and the stubborn patience to get there.

That feeling was new. I really liked it.

## Three Weeks

That's how long the whole thing took from finalizing the script to a finished film. Three weeks. For a team of engineers with zero film experience.

We couldn't just explain CROW. That was never going to work. The product was too technical and the attention it deserved was too high to throw away on a standard feature walkthrough. The direction I took was to make people feel something first, give them characters to care about, a problem to follow, a resolution that actually lands, and let the product earn its place in the story. The team trusted that and made it happen.

That's what CROW got. Not a product demo. An actual story.

And that's how I ended up solving the biggest crisis Project CROW ever had. lol
